Simple Statistical Analysis With Python

by Count Rugen

Posted on May 18, 2018

It’s sometimes handy to see if there’s a significant relationship between two things in your life. Perhaps it’s calories eaten and weight lost; or maybe it’s the P/E ratio of a stock and it’s subsequent 1-year growth. Who knows.

It’s actually pretty easy to use the scipy library for python to do some statistical analysis.

Linear Regression

Here’s an example piece of code that evaluates the linear relationship between x and y, and then evaluates the linear relationship between x and ln(y). See scipy.stats.linregress.

Graphing the log of y, flattens out exponential growth curves so that we can use linear analysis on them.

#!/usr/bin/python
from scipy import stats
import math

#Simple linear regression analysis
x = [list of x values]
y = [list of corresponding y values]

if len(x) > 0 and len(y) > 0:
    slope, intercept, R, p, stderr = stats.linregress(x,y)

#Now let's take the natural log of y instead
yy = []
for value in y:
    yy.append(math.log(y))

if len(x) > 0 and len(yy) > 0:
    slope, intercept, R, p, stderr = stats

Of course, you’ll have to take care not to take the log of zero, or negative numbers; that sort of thing.

The slope and intercept give you the values of the formula: y = slope*x + intercept for the linear regression line.

R is a correlation coefficient that tells you how well the data fit the line (1.0 = perfect fit). The p value gives you the odds that R is an illusion and the data just happen to look like they form a pattern by coincidence. In science, a p-value less than 0.05 is considered statistically significant.

Comparing two populations

Let’s say you aren’t looking for a linear relationship, but you want to see if something makes a difference. Maybe you want to see if taking vitamin D at all changed something else in your life that you can measure. You might have a bunch of measurements from days when you didn’t take vitamin D and days when you did.

Let’s say that the averages for these two groups of measurements are different. Does that mean something, or is that just coincidence? Statistics can help answer that question.

These groups of measurements are essentially two different populations and we can use the sort of statistical analysis used in biology of pharmaceuticals to see what the odds are that the differences we observe are real.

One such test is the Welch’s T-Test. See scipy.stats.ttest_ind.

#!/usr/bin/python
from scipy import stats

A = [list of values from population A]
B = [list of values from population B]

tstat, p = stats.ttest_ind(A,B,axis=0,equal_var=False)

Here tstat is the t-statistic which tells you basically how different the two populations probably are. It’s pretty much the ratio of how different two groups are from each other over how different members of each group are from other members of the same group. Or: [inter-group differences] / [intra-group differences].

Again, the p-value tells us that chance that this difference is really due to random chance. And a p-value under 0.05 means we have a statistically significant difference between the populations (but it doesn’t tell us how big that difference is; that’s what the R value is for).
