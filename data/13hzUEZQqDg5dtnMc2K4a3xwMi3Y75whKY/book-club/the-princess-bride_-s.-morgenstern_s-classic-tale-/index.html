Westley’s Review

Fun story: My sister read this book when I was a wee one. She mentioned the forward where the author explains that his Father read him the original story when he was a boy and then he tracked it down one day in the library, and found it was full of boring passages on the economics, politics, etc. of the two cities.

Apparently, his Father had cut out those parts when he read it to him. So the author decided to cut out those boring parts and publish the good-parts version: The Princess Bride.

For years, I remembered this story. One day, I tried tracking down a copy of The Princess Bride by S. Morgan Stern. I couldn’t find one; only Goldman’s adaptation.

Years later, my thoughts turned to that book once again, and I returned to the topic. This time I learned that that story at the beginning of the book was bullshit. The author made it up to frame what would come next. There is no S. Morgan Stern. Goldman… I love that guy!

Amazon.com Review

The Princess Bride is a true fantasy classic. William Goldman describes it as a “good parts version” of “S. Morgenstern’s Classic Tale of True Love and High Adventure.” Morgenstern’s original was filled with details of Florinese history, court etiquette, and Mrs. Morgenstern’s mostly complimentary views of the text. Much admired by academics, the “Classic Tale” nonetheless obscured what Mr. Goldman feels is a story that has everything: “Fencing. Fighting. Torture. Poison. True love. Hate. Revenge. Giants. Hunters. Bad men. Good men. Beautifulest ladies. Snakes. Spiders. Beasts of all natures and descriptions. Pain. Death. Brave men. Coward men. Strongest men. Chases. Escapes. Lies. Truths. Passion. Miracles.”

Goldman frames the fairy tale with an “autobiographical” story: his father, who came from Florin, abridged the book as he read it to his son. Now, Goldman is publishing an abridged version, interspersed with comments on the parts he cut out.

Is The Princess Bride a critique of classics like Ivanhoe and The Three Musketeers , that smother a ripping yarn under elaborate prose? A wry look at the differences between fairy tales and real life? Simply a funny, frenetic adventure? No matter how you read it, you’ll put it on your “keeper” shelf. --Nona Vero
