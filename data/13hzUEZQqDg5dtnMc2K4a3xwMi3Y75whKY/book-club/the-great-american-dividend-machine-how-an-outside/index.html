Prince Humperdinck’s Review

Did not like.

Now I can’t say I gave this book every opportunity to impress me, but I think I gave it enough of a chance to impress, and it failed.

The first few lines of the book were actually pretty engaging and I was excited to learn the details. Unfortunately, that seems to be where the book falls short.

I quickly began skimming through loads of fluff, anecdotes, and vague descriptions devoid of specifics. Maybe all the specifics were there and I missed them. But if you have to wade through a ton of fluff to find any useful content, then a book is a stinker in my opinion. Even if there’s burried gold in there somewhere, the author shouldn’t make it hard to find in the first place.

As far as I can tell, the author doesn’t actually have a system to promote; just some vague advice and a general focus on dividend investing. Oh, but he does have a subscription service to promote; that becomes clear very early on!

Scanning this book also came on the heals of reading the far better Get Rich with Dividends; a book that wades through a lot of explaining the basics (which is to be expected) and a few personal anecdotes (forgivable) before pretty much laying out the fine details of the system in a straight-forward manner.

The Great American Dividend Machine, by contrast, seemed to be much more of an advertisement for the author’s subscription stock-picking service.
