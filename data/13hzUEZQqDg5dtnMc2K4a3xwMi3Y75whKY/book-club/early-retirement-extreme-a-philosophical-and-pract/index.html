Prince Humperdinck’s Review

There’s some good stuff here; lot’s of useful equations.

Fisker has an interesting tactic. He uses your savings ratio , the amount of your income that you save, as the basis for most calculations. So instead of calculating how much you need for retirement and how long it will take to save it, you would calculate how much of your income you can afford to live off of.

From your savings ratio, we then calculated useful things. For example, using the 4% rule (you can safely withdraw 4% a year in retirement), it naturally follows that whatever your savings rate is, you’d need to save enough for that 4% to equal whatever is left over.

  • (spending ratio)/0.04 = 25*spending ratio = how much you need to save for retirement
  • spending ratio = 1-(savings ratio)
  • therefore: 25*(1-savings ratio) = how much you need to save for retirement

Similarly, how long it takes to reach 25*(1-savings ratio) depends a lot on what the savings ratio is. If Y = the number of years, then:

  • 25*(1-savings ratio) = Y * savings ratio
  • Y = 25*(1-savings ratio)/(savings ratio)

If your savings ratio is 0.5 (50%), then Y = 25 years; If your savings ratio is 0.75, then Y = 8 years, 4 months.

This assumes no growth of your savings due to investmests, compounding, etc.

Fisker has some investing advice, but mostly his thing is aggressive savings made possible by extreme frugality. He shows mathematically, that in the sort run, if you’re saving aggressively, then your investing returns don’t matter that much; it’s more about how much of your income you save.

There’s some philosophy I guess; you can just skim over his ramblings if they bore you. Honestly, I don’t remember the book being very pholosophical; probably because I skimmed through the stuff that wasn’t of interest to me.

That’s what I suggest you do with all personal finance books; skim the author’s anecdotes and backstory (unless you find them interesting) and skip around. After you’ve read a few personal finance books, you’ll see that they all feel compelled to cover some of the basics… which after a few books, you’ll already know.

I reccomend this book for anyone, and highly recommend it for anyone interested in early retirement; just realize you’ll probably be skipping over a lot of uninteresting parts to get to the good stuff.

Description

Early Retirement Extreme provides a robust strategy that makes it possible to stop working for money in just a short number of years. It provides a paradigm shift in economic perspective from consuming to producing. Your value to society is not how much you earn or how much you buy. It is what you create and produce for yourself and for others. It is what you leave, not what you take. Consumers are often locked into expensive options, but producers have the flexibility to create appropriate solutions at a quarter of the cost. The resulting savings (the difference between income and expenses) is one’s monetary contribution to society. When savings are put to work through investments, society will pay dividends which cover the remaining expenses resulting in financial independence. The strategy can also be used to pay off debt, travel the world, volunteer, go back to school, or work on otherwise nonprofitable endeavors without worrying about the next paycheck. It offers a compelling alternative to the default choice of graduating high school, getting a college degree, buying a car, getting married, buying a house, filling it with furniture, clothes, TVs, washing machines, lawn mowers, and electric egg boilers, and then spending the next 40 years working 9-5 to pay it all off.
