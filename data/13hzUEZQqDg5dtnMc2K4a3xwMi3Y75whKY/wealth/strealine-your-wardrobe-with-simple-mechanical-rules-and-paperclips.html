Strealine Your Wardrobe With a Simple Rule-Based Method... and Paperclips

by Westley

Posted on May 27, 2018

I was inspired by a section of the book Early Retirement Extreme where the author talks about streamlining your life by getting rid of unused possession. The basic idea was that by having fewer possessions, you can save money by living in a smaller place and also get some money back by selling your possessions.

I was re-organizing my closet today and came up with a scheme for streamlining my wardrobe that works by following simple mechanical rules twice a year.

The simple preparation

  1. For each category of clothing, have 1, 2, or 3 boxes, drawers, or whatever for the clothes.
    • I used old cardboard boxes that I had left over from moving. So far, my closet has:
      • 2 T-shirt boxes
      • 1 Polo-shirt box
    • 2 Pant boxes
    • 1 Sweater box
  2. Have one box for clothes to get rid of (donate, sell, trash, whatever)
  3. If you only have one box or drawer of something for one of the categories, and you’re happy with that fine; no need to sreamline those clothes. But for clothes you have a lot of (people give me shirts all the time, but never pants), sort the clothes you have so that:
    • Box 1 has the items you like the most
    • Box 2 has the items you like less
    • Box 3 has the items you like even less
  4. Put a paper clip somewhere on every item of clothing in the last box; the one with your least-favorite clothes
  5. If there’s anything you’re ready to get rid of, put it in the get-rid-of box right away
  6. Set a calendar reminder for 6 months later

For the next six months, if you wear anything from box 2, you’ll naturally take the paper clip off it first. Don’t put it back on. You don’t need to worry about where you put washed clothes, any box with space in it is fine; just not the get-rid-of box, of course.

If you decide to wear something in the get-rid-of box, that’s OK too. Just put it back in box 1 or box 2 after you wash it.

The semiannual routine

Every six months thereafter, do the following:

  1. First get rid of any clothes still in the get-rid-of box
  2. Go through least-favorite box for each category of clothing
    • Put anything that still has a paperclip on it in the (now empty) get-rid-of box (remove the paperclip first)
  3. Resort your boxes putting your favorites in box 1 and progressively less favorites in boxes 2, 3, etc.
  4. Tag all of the clothes in your least-favorite boxes with paper clips

The result

The result will be that clothes that you haven’t worn in six months will work their way to the get-rid-of box. Maybe not all the clothes you haven’t worn in six months, but any of your least favorite clothes will.

Then any of those clothes that still aren’t worn for another six months are finally gotten rid of. It’ll take a whole year for that to happen, so you’ll be pretty certain that you really aren’t going to miss them when they’re gone.

Eventually, you’ll whittle your way down to either just the clothes you wear often, or just one box or drawer (or whatever) for each category. If you want, you can get smaller boxes and keep the system going, or you can just be happy with things the way they are.

And if you use cardboard boxes (a super cheap, but not very stylish option), then you’ll have an easier time moving if you ever have to.

Special note

I found the section in Early Retirement Extreme about depriciation especially interesting. The author suggest that you think of all of your possessions as businesses do: as constantly depriciating. In essence, all of your possessions have some value that declines as time goes on. You could sell that brand new TV for not that much less than you bought if for, but after five years, you won’t be able to sell it for nearly enough.

Your true cost of owning anything, is the difference between the price you paid when you bought it, and the price you get for it when you sell it. Quickly selling something soon after you decide it isn’t worth much means there’s a chance your true cost of owning it will be much less than you paid for it. But hold on to it until it depreciates to having no worth (or throw it away), and you’ve locked in your true cost at exactly how much you paid for it plus whatever you paid to store it, maintain it, whatever.

Yeah, depreciation doesn’t apply that much to clothing, but it’s an interesting section of the book and I recommend you give it a read.
