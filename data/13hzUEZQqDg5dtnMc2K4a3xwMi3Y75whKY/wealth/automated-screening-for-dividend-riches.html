Automated Screening For Dividend Riches

by Prince Humperdinck

Posted on April 20, 2018

A while ago, I reviewed a book called Get Rich with Dividends. In it, the author presents some sensible criteria for finding stable dividend-producing investments.

The basic criteria for choosing investments are:

  • Dividend Yield >= 4.7%
  • Average Annualized Dividend Growth over the last 10 years >= 10%
  • Payout Ratio: Annual Dividends / Free Cash Flow per Share < 75% (Except for MLPs and REITs)

I recently came accross a similar book on dividend growth investing called The Single Best Investment by Lowell Miller. In it, the author advocates using an earnings-per-shared-based Payout Ratio of 0.60. Miller also suggests that FCF tends to be larger than Earnings, so I’ve decided to use the higher of the two cut-offs (0.75) for Payout Ratios based on EPS and the smaller (0.60) as a cut-off for Payout Ratios based on FCF.

Automating the Screening Process

Luckily, you can download a spreadsheet with lists of stocks with positive dividend growth over the last 5-10 years at dripinvesting.org. The spreadsheet is updated monthly.

Using the xlrd library for python, I created my own screener that downloads the U.S.DividendChampions spreadsheet and spits out stocks and REITs that match the criteria.

I filtered out MLPs because they’re such a hassle come tax time. Also, you can’t own MLPs in a tax-sheltered account such as in IRA.

Free Cash Flow is a Hassle

Free cash flow is pretty easy to calculate using the Income from Operations - Capital Expenses method, but there’s no longer an automated method to grab these numbers through python (there used to be, but it’s broken). So to calculate the Free Cash Flow, I look up those numbers in Yahoo finance.

However, the spreadsheet does include Earnings per Share, which is a convenient alternative to Free Cash Flow that we can use to calculate the Payout Ratio.

So as a quick preliminary screen, I filter out any non-REITs that have a dividends-per-share/earnings-per-share ratio of 0.675 or greater. Then I manually look up the numbers for the free cash flow and keep them stored in a database so I don’t have to calculated them for the same stock more than once a year.

Adding a 5-Year Filter

The spreadsheet also gives us the 5-year annualized dividend growth for stocks, and I decided to limit the search to stocks with both a 10-year dividend growth of 10% or more and a 5-year dividend growth of 10% or more as well.

As you might imagine, there aren’t many stocks that survive the selection process. The criteria are pretty strict.

Lowering Standards

I decided create some lower-levels of search criteria.

Any stocks that meet the original standards, I call Moonrakers. But stocks with the same 10% 5 and 10-year dividend growth, but a lower yield are my Topgallants.

The 4.7% yield is a little arbitrary. I does the job of placing a dividend yield squarely above the average inflation rate since 1960, but it doesn’t suggest where other ranges might be.

Inflation Rate

Knowing that the 4.7% yield criteria was related to inflation, I chose to use it’s ratio to inflation as a guide.

With the easymoney python library, I can calculate the average annualized inflation rate since 1960 (the earliest year that the easymoney library can get inflation data from). With that, I found that the average inflation rate was about 3.78% (vs the 3.4% figure in the book, which probably goes back further than 1960). A 4.7% yield is about 25% greater than the average inflation rate, so I decided that this would be my cut-off for the Moonrakers.

A natural cut-off for the Topgallants then, would be half of that; 12.5%. And the lowest cut-off for yield then would be the average inflation rate since 1960.

This gives the possible benefit of having the cut-off for yield respond to changes in inflation over time.

Yield Cut-Offs

  • Moonrakers: 1.25 * Avg. Inflation
  • Topgallants: 1.125 * Avg. Inflation
  • Absolute Bottom: Avg. Inflation

Topsails and Rising Stars

I created two more tiers of screening results.

Topsails yield more than the average inflation rate since 1960 and have 5-year and 10-year history of 10% annualized dividend growth.

Rising Stars yield more than the average inflation rate since 1960 and have a 5-year annualized divided growth of 10% or more. However, their 10-year annualized dividend growth may be as low as the average inflation rate since 1960.

But Wait… Someone Was Left Out

At some point, I realized that I have left out a possible group of stocks that would have made it through the original screen.

Stocks with a yield at least 25% greater that inflation and a 10-year dividend growth of at least 10% but a 5-year dividend growth rate of less than 10% would not show up in any of the screens.

I decided to call these ones the Cruisers as they show signs that their dividend growth may be slowing down. If the yield is good, then these may be stable companies that continue to pump out dividends year after year.

And One Last Category

OK, because python has some nice statistical packages (numpy) and because looking at the performers in the top percentiles is sometimes useful, I developed one more category. The Jolly Rodgers are equities that fell in the 50th percentile for yield, 5-year dividend growth, and 10-year dividend growth but were not picked up by any of the other scans.

N.B. These picks are limited to at least matching the average inflation rate since 1960 in their yield, and 5 and 10-year dividend growths.
