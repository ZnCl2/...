Canadian Patent # 475279

Method of Producing Ketene Derivatives

My invention relates to the production of unsaturated bodies such as ketenes and ketene derivatives from materials such as ethyl alcohol, its anhydride, ethyl ether, ethylene, acetylene, acetaldehyde, etc.

I have established that the products resulting from the practice of my invention are beneficial for medicinal purposes, and are harmless in the treatment of humans when used by physicians who have been instructed properly.

One method for the practice of my invention is as follows:

Assuming that the starting material is acetaldehyde, this material is added very slowly, as by being carried in as a gas by admixture with the gas acetylene, to strong sulfuric acid, preferably 95% or stronger, or phosphoric acid, 85% or stronger the acid being well cooled. The proportion of added acetaldehyde is preferably less than 20% by volume of the amount of acid employed.

This mixture, which I shall designate as product A, is allowed to age until sulfurous acid is formed, and then submitted to distillation with a sweep gas such as nitrogen to remove the products.

The distillate is condensed by cooling, neutralized with chalk, then extracted with chloroform. The extract is washed with water.

Both the chloroform extract and the water washings are evaporated to dryness, and the residues, after dilution with water used for intravenous, or intramuscular injection.

As an alternative, the product A is submitted to neutralization with chalk, then extracted with chloroform and this extract washed with water. Both the chloroform extract and the water washing are evaporated and after proper dilution with water, used for intravenous or intramuscular injection.

The products resulting from the practice of my process are various polymers of ketenes. The product which is soluble in chloroform carries a structure of conjugated carbonyl groups and no carboxyls. The product which is soluble in water carries a conjugated system of ethylene and carbonyl groups.

Canadian Patent # 420739

Unsaturated Compound Preparation

My invention is directed to a method or process for producing a group of unsaturated compounds possessing free valences with catalytic activity, and more specifically is directed to the production of such substances photochemically.

In the practice of my invention an aqueous solution of formaldehyde ad an aqueous solution of slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) are mixed, about equal parts by volume of fairly concentrated solutions of formaldehyde and of lime are employed.

The concentrations of these two materials are not critical, but I prefer to employ fairly saturated or moderately concentrated solutions.

After the mixture has been thoroughly or well shaken, it is placed in a flask transparent to or containing a port transparent to ultraviolet light. The source of the ultraviolet light may be the sun or an artificial source.

The mixture is exposed to the ultraviolet light until test portions when evaporated produce a syrup that crystallizes and contains only a trace or small percent of formaldehyde. The time required for this step of the process depends upon the intensity of the uv light. I have found it varies from one to 3 days.

The resultant changed mixture is then evaporated in vacuo in a stream of air until a syrup remain, which is filtered through a sterile glass filter plate.

Portions of the material are then diluted with sterile water to one to a thousand or higher and sealed in sterile ampoules.

This invention relates to the production of unsaturated bodies from sugars of the hexose class, namely, glucose, fructose and similar substances, and such of their decompositio  products as lend themselves to the present process, such as acetaldehyde, paraldehyde, malonic acid, lactic acid, glyceric acid, glyceric aldehyde, pyruvic acid, pyruvic aldehyde, ethyl alcohol, ethyl ether, glycolic acid, glycolic acid, and glyoxal.

According to the present invention, oxidation of these substances is caused to proceed as a peroxidation of unsaturated groups which are produced by the removal of water.

In carrying on the process according to the present invention, the first step in the oxidation of fructose consists in the removal of hydroxyl from the carbon atom which is in beta position to the carbonyl group and the removal of the hydrogen attached to the carbon in alpha position to the carbonyl group, or vice versa, as a molecule of water. The unsaturated group that is produced is able to add oxygen (activated oxygen) and undergo cleavage at this point with the formation of two carbonyl groups. Thus fructose is split into two, three-carbon chains which may undergo change to lactic acid and related substance, which, in turn, are ale to undergo loss of water with the production of unsaturated bodies of ketene structure.

In the same way glucose, by losing hydroxyl and hydrogen in the beta and alpha positions, respectively, to the carbonyl group undergoes a desaturation which can add activated oxygen ad cleave to form two keto groups, one on a two-carbon chain and the other on a four-carbon chain. By repeating the same process on the four carbon chain, three two-carbon chain groupings are produced which by further desaturation yield ketenes. The positioning of the initial desaturations is accomplished through the catalysis of a halogen, preferably iodine as such or in combination, such as calcium iodide, or an organic form of iodine in which the carrier is a benzene ring or in which an alipathic chain with or without nitrogen as carrier, such, for example, as an iodide compound of creatine.

Active dehydration is produced by a strong mineral acid such as a mixture of fuming and strong sulfuric acid or a comparably strong phosphoric acid.

In carrying on the present process, dry glucose or dry fructose are mixed with an excess of strong acid containing the selected halogen catalyst.  The amount required is preferably less than one-hundredth of one percent of the amount of sugar used. As to the phosphoric acid, I prefer to employ a mixture of phosphoric anhydride and 85% phosphoric acid in such proportions of each that the mixture contains the minimum amount of water and is still able to flow. The catalyst is added to the acid and to this is added the dry pulverized sugar. The mixture is covered with a stopper and allowed to stand at about body temperature until the sugar is apparently fully dissolved. The mixture is then warmed gradually to about around 100 C over a flame with which it does not have direct contact, the mixture being well shaken to distribute the heat and prevent overheating of an part until it reaches a deep burgundy color in which a column, a centimeter in thickness, is almost opaque. The quicker the heating the sooner it reaches the color mentioned. I have found, however, that it is better to conduct the process slowly, taking 2 or 3 hours for its accomplishment.

It would appear that the dehydrated sugar has, through its free valences, activated oxygen present and become a peroxide and cleavage has taken place. Some of the desaturated substance under the influence of peroxide has polymerized. As the desaturation and peroxidation and cleavage have resulted in shorter chains of 2 and 3 carbon atoms each and further desaturation is accomplished, the desaturated bodies for peroxides which catalyze further polymerization of the unsaturated bodies formed. The polymers thus produced may be cleaved into their component parts by high dilution with water or preferably by destructive distillation. I place the brown mixture in a flask that is connected with a condenser and pass a stream of gas, such as carbon dioxide, through the apparatus while the mixture is being heated, and below the temperature at which decomposition is so fast as to produce inconvenient foaming, so that the products can be carried over before undergoing too much decomposition.

In the condensed product is found a mixture of substances containing modified products of the unsaturated substances obtained by the destructive distillation of the polymers obtained by the destructive distillation of the polymers and also in equilibrium with them the unsaturated substances desired in sufficient concentration for further dilution and use for treatment of diseases.

As mentioned above, my process is directed to the production of unsaturated bodies from hexose and hexose decomposition products. It is to be understood, also, that fuming and concentrated sulfuric acid may be substituted for the strong acid referred to.

The product obtained by any of the aforesaid processes is diluted with water as above specified and may be sealed in ampoules for hypodermic injection, for the treatment of cancer and various infections.

British Patent # 1,193,703

Process for the Production of Triquinoyl and its Polymers


The invention relates to a process for the production of triquinoyl and its polymers having therapeutic properties.

According to the invention there is provided a process for the production of a solution containing triquinoyl or a mixture thereof with its polymers, which process comprises chlorinating an aqueous solution of inositol or a mixture therof with its polymers, which process comprises chlorinating an aqueous solution of inositol in the presence of light of 40 C to 60 C, treating the product with barium chloride to obtain barium rhodizonate, treating said rhodizonate with sulfuric acid to obtain rhodizonic acid, and treating the acid so obtained with manganese dioxide.

The amount of manganese dioxide is controlled in accordance with the product which it is desired to obtain.

The amount can be controlled so as to regulate the reaction and in this way it is possible to obtain a substantially complete oxidation of the rhodizonic acid to triquinoyl without further change to the compound. The amount can, however, be further adjusted in order that such change does occur whereby proportions of the polymers are obtained.

Therefore, according to a feature of the invention, the amount of manganese dioxide may be controlled so as to obtain triquinoyl as the product.

Alternatively the amount of manganese dioxide with which the rhodizonic acid is treated is so adjusted as to obtain conversion of the triquinoyl to its polymers.

According to a preferred feature residual impurities are filtered off from the product.

The solution of inositol may be a 1% solution.

According to a further preferred feature, chlorination is continued until the temperature of the solution falls to ambient temperature. When this temperature is reached, the reaction solution is preferably maintained in darkness until the formation of a dark-colored precipitate and the evolution of a gas have finished.

The solution containing triquinoyl and its polymers preferably has added thereto additional material which is (i)nickel sulphate, cobalt sulfate, manganese sulfate, or molybdenum sulfate and (ii)ferric sulfate, cupric sulfate, or zinc sulfate, the zinc being in its higher oxidation state.

It has been found that the compound produced by the process of the invention has therapeutically valuable properties and can be used everywhere where oxidation and/or reduction agents are utilizable to cure illnesses. Furthermore the compound is useful for blocking electron transfer.

The following Examples illustrate the invention:

Example I

Chlorine gas is passed into a 1% aqueous solution of inositol which is simultaneously exposed to the light of a daylight lamp or to bright sunlight at a temperature of from 40 C to 60 C. This is maintained until the temperature has returned to room temperature. The reaction solution is kept in the dark until the formation of gas is finished and a black precipitate has formed.

The solution is filtered and the filtrate neutralized with sodium bicarbonate and treated with barium chloride, whereby barium rhodizonate and a small amount of polymerized barium salt compounds precipitate as a reddish precipitate which precipitate is separated by filtration.

The precipitate of barium rhodizonate is treated with dilute sulfuric acid whereby the barium compound is converted to barium sulfate which can be filtered off leaving a filtrate which is a very weak acid solution, which is then treated with manganese dioxide.

The amount of manganese dioxide added determines the composition of the product. The amount may be controlled so that the rhodizonic acid is oxidized to triquinoyl, but may be varied, if desired, to obtain proportions of the polymers of triquinoyl. Any impurities, such as manganese dioxide or sulfates, present in the product can be removed, for example by filtration to give a solution which is designated solution I herein.

The solution filtered off from the reddish precipitate as mentioned hereinbefore, is treated with sulfuric acid to precipitate barium sulfate. The latter is removed by filtration. The resulting clear solution, designated as solution II herein, contains a small amount of triquinoyl and its polymers and, in addition, high polymers of rhodizonic acid.

Example II

The solution obtained in accordance with Example I containing triquinoyl and its polymers. i.e., solution I, is added to solution II. The solution obtained is designated herein as solution III. Both solutions show themselves to be good reducing agents directly in the cold with Fehling’s and Benedict’s solutions. A quantitative test of solution III showed, with reference to its power as a reducing agent on Fehling’s solution, that the solution III contained approximately 1% of reducing substances.

Example III

1 ml of solution III is diluted with sterile distilled water to 10 ml. Each cubic centimeter of solution then contains about 1 mg of reducing substances. If 1 ml of the solution III is diluted with 1 liter of sterile distilled water then the concentration of reducing substances is 1 microgram per ml. This solution is designated as solution IV.

Example IV

Since the molecular weights of triquinoyl and its polymers are rather high it is advantageous if the amount of oxidizing and/or reducing substances is increased by substances having a lower molecular weight such as glyoxal or methyl glyoxal. For this purpose 1 liter of solution III is treated with 1 mg of glyoxal, 1 mg of methyl glyoxal, and 1 mg of p-benzoquinone. This solution is designated as solution V.

Example V

It has been shown that the cations of some metals in their higher oxidation state assist the oxidation and/or reduction powers of carbonyl compounds in tissue exchanges where an electron transport is required from the substrata to the carbonyl compound and from the carbonyl compound to an electron receiver on the way to acid. For this reason 1 liter of the solution V is t reated with 1 microgram of nickel sulfate or molybdenum sulfate and 1 mg of an iron sulfate, copper sulfate, or zinc sulfate where the last-mentioned metals are in their higher oxidation states.

Example VI

Solution V is adjusted to pH  by adding sulfuric acid and drawn through a sintered bacteria filter of Pyrex glass and put into ampoules in aseptic conditions and used as an oxidizing and/or reduction means in illnesses in which there is a blocking of the electron transfer.
