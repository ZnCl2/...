Daily Courier News (Elgin, IL), Friday, December 22, 1978 ---

No-Gas Engine Ready for Showing

by

Mike Bailey

An engine which reportedly operates on electricity generated from chemical reaction, first previewed in the Daily Courier news more than a year ago, is now on display to the public.

The inventor, who still requests anonymity for security reasons, says there will be a public demonstration of the engine soon, perhaps as early as Wednesday.

Early this morning, the heavy blinds which closed off the interior of Magnatron, inc., 161 S. Grove Ave., Elgin, from the rest of the world were raised.

Behind the plate glass window is the chassis of an old car, set up on blocks.

And inside the engine housing is a motor completely unfamiliar in size, shape, and description.

That engine is the one described in the Daily Courier News on Aug. 12, 1977.

Its inventor claimed it was a revolutionary new source of power derived from the chemical reaction of deuterium and gallium.

That controlled reaction results in the fusion of two atoms forming a new atom, he says. In the process, electricity is released and that is what powers the engine, the inventor claims.

The engine emits no pollutants and uses no fossil fuel in its operation. The only lubricant is synthetically produced and the engine needs no petroleum products for operation, the inventor claims.

While the inventor will not detail the exact operation, he says that 17 ounces of deuterium, a hydrogen product which can be manufactured, and an ounce and a half of gallium, a heavy metal which is a well known donor source for electricity, is all that is needed to power the engine for 100,000 miles.

The flow of the gallium and deuterium is controlled by magnetic tunnels and where to two meet or strike, a laser beam forces their fusion.

The inventor says numerous problems have delayed his original target date of late 1977 for display of the engine.

But after a few setbacks and some unforeseen problems, he says this first production model is ready and functioning.

The engine is not in mass production yet, and will not be until funding is obtained, he said early this week.

The engine can be seen from the sidewalk in front of the shop.

The inventor says that when he has the demonstration of the motor, investors and prospective Magnatron customers may present themselves.

The engine is in operation, the inventor says, however, it is not hooked up to the car’s transmission for safety reasons, he claims.

In a closed demonstration attended by the Daily Courier News, the engine was turned on, but as it was not hooked up to the car’s transmission, it was not possible to verify its power, reported to be 525 horsepower.

There did not appear to be an external power supply and a regular car battery used to power the car’s accessories was disconnected on request. Removing the battery did not cause any noticeable depreciation of power.

“They’ve had me convinced for a long time. I’ve been hearing about this for quite a while. We know someone who’s getting a dealership”.

“They made a believer out of me”, he said.

“I’ve been interested since I first heard about it. I’ve been convinced for a long time that it actually works”, said M. Christensen of rural Elgin.

“I have lots of doubts about this”, said Brian Getzelman of Elgin.

“I read the story in the paper and I’m even more skeptical now that I’ve seen it”, said Paul Larson of Elgin.

“I can’t believe these small bottle contain enough fuel to power a car for that distance. The fact that I didn’t get to see it run really had a negative effect on me”, he said.

Johnson is undaunted by the non-believers and said his next step is to put together 25 engines which he will submit for testing and demonstration.

He mentioned the federal Bureau of Standards, Underwriter’s Laboratory and Ralph Nader’s group as possibilities to conduct tests.

But he admitted to one interviewer that people may be skeptical about investing in his engine until those tests are complete.
