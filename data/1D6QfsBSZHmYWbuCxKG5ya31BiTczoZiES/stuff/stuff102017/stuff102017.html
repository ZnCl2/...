Links Lindbergh and Lanphier with Production of Magnetic Motor by Guggenheim Group

NY, Feb 26, AP -- The New York American in a copyrighted article will say tomorrow that Col. Charles A. Lindbergh or Maj. Thomas Lanphier has brought to New York the electro-magnetic motor of Lester Hendershot for purposes of perfection and production.

The motor will be taken to the office of the Guggenheim Fund for the Promotion of Aetronatics, the paper will say.

"Despite protestations of Lindbergh , H.K. Knight and others of the party that they were in New York on a pleasure jaunt and they positively would have nothing to do York Tuesday to continue negotiations with Lanphier, Lindbergh and the Guggenheim representatives, the paper will say.

Tells of Engine

In commenting on the invention, the New York American will say that Hendershot said, "The engine actually makes its own electricity. There is a magnet which acts as a starter. From that point on it generates its own power. The magnet will not wear out under 2000 hours, nearer 3000.

"And, contrary to reports already circulated, the motor is not the size of a vacuum cleaner motor. it is 28 inches long. It weighs 130 pounds. it has developed 45 horsepower at 1800 rpm, the average speed of internal combustion motors.

Denies Knowing Plans. Motor Inventor, However, Says He Will Go to New York Tuesday.

Pittsburgh, PA, Feb. 26 (AP) -- Lester J. Hendershot of West Elizabeth, PA, inventor of the "fuelless motor," today denied all knowledge of a corporation in process of formation by Detroit friends to exploit his creation, but made known that he will go to New York Tuesday to confer with Col. The model of the motor that was on display in Detroit last week has been sent to New York, Hendershot said.

Going into detail regarding the machine, the inventor said it was developed while he was experimenting in an attempt to produce an improved compass and he asserted the force that "turns my motor is the same force that pulls the needle of a compass around."

Hendershot who is a licensed pilot, started experimenting 3 years ago when he first took up flying. The motor, he said, first took shape a year ago.


Minneapolis Star (28 Feb, 1928)
Government Scientists Turn Thumbs Down On Contention Of Fuelless Motor Inventor
'Can't Get Something for Nothing,' Asserts Compass Expert

by Robert S. Thornburgh

Pittsburgh, Feb 28 -- "It works."

This was the reply of Lester Hendershot to published statements that scientists doubted the ability of his fuelless motor to draw power from the earth's magnetic forces sufficient to turn an airplane motor.

Hendershot said he did not care to reveal the secret of his motor, and would not do so to "satisfy the curiosity" of doubters.

Washington, Feb. 28. (INS) -- Terrestrial magnetism may be harnessed to create energy that will turn wheels and make telegraph instruments tick, a group of government scientists agreed today, but they turned thumbs down on the mysterious and much discussed "fuelless motor" invented by Lester J. Hendershot of Pittsburgh.

That such a motor or generator can be used to drive airplanes was regarded as extremely doubtful by Dr Paul R. Heyl, inventor of the earth inductor compass that guided Lindbergh to France, and Dr. H.D. Hubbard, who has visualized apparent impossibilities as a camera which focuses automatically as does the human eye, and interplanetary transportation.

Device Would Be Too Heavy

"You can't get something for nothing," declared Dr. Heyl, whose inductor compass operates on a principle somewhat similar to that of the Hendershot generator.

"To operate an airplane requires at least 400 horsepower. A device that would develop that power from earth currents would be too heavy and cumbersome for aircraft.

"If Hendershot's generator operates a motor or lights a light, the whole story of the invention has not been told. From the newspaper dispatches I am at a loss to understand what kind of a machine it is. I can conceive of no practical device that would develop sufficient power from earth magnetism to run an airplane."

Principle of Compass

The earth inductor compass is based on the principle that the voltage of a direct current dynamo is changed by shifting the angle between the magnetic field and the brushes, which collect the current from a revolving armature. The armature is driven by a wind wheel, whose motion is dependent upon the movement of the airplane.

While there are few "impossibles" in Dr Hubbard's vocabulary, he said that a "fuelless motor" that would drive an airplane "looks impossible."

"It's a fact that there in the bureau, the light of the north star, millions of light miles away in space, has been so directed as to turn a small mirror," Hubbard said.

"A device was used in the war that would detect the presence of a man within 50 feet by the heat given off by his face. But that doesn't mean that the north star's radiation would charge a storage battery, or that water could be boiled by the heat of a man's face."

Effect of Currents

Hubbard explained some remarkable manifestations of the effect of earth currents, supposed to be the motive power of the new generator. In periods of sun spot maximum it has been found possible to operate telegraph instruments all over eastern United States and in England without batteries, simply from power generated by magnetic storms.

In theory the magnetic storms light lights since they are responsible of the mysterious phenomenon of the aurora -- the blazing electric system of the frozen north.

"But to say that the comparatively weak magnetic field of the earth, which turns the compass needle, will develop in a generator sufficient energy to drive an airplane motor, is too much, and proof of the airplane in flight must be furnished before the invention is taken seriously," Hubbard said.

Engine Declared Chance Discovery

Pittsburgh, Feb. 28 -- Lester J. Hendershot, West Elizabeth inventor who startled the motor industry with the announcement of his electromagnetic engine, really started out to devise a new type of compass, D. Barr Peet, his partner, said today.

Hendershot discovered by cutting the magnetic lines of force he could develop a rotary motion of a premagnetized core. This motion could be stabilized in the manner the motor was built, thus contructing an engine with a predetermined number of revolutions per minutre.

"A constant speed motor is one of the greatest needs of aviation," said Peet. "This motor can be built to any speed desired, and will last indefinitely. The motor Hendershot demonstrated in Detroit was constructed to turn over 1800 rpm, and developed 45 horsepower on a block test."

Denies Radio Phenomena

Peet was emphatic in his denial that there was any radio phenomena connected with the motor. It generates no heat, he explained, and could only be utilized as electrical current if attached to a motor generator.

The partner explained that Hendershot originally was seeking to develop a compass that would be more reliable than the ones presently used by aviators. He was trying to improve upon the magnetic induction compass that indicates true north.

"Hendershot found that with a premagnetized core, he could set up a magnetized field that would indicate the true north, but after he found this, he could find no way of utilizing it in the compass he had set out to devise," said Peet.

Developed Rotary Motion

"In continuing his experiments, Hendershot learned that by cutting the same line of magnetic force from north and south, he had an indicator of the true north. By cutting the magnetic field east and west he found he could develop a rotary motion.

"Hendershot now has a motor similar to an enlarged magneto, as the revolving armature passes the magnetic poles of the magnet, current enters the end of the armature wrapping, coming out of the other end highly increased in voltage.

"The magnetic core in the armature acts as a starter. From that point the motor creates its own energy."

Peet, who is manager of Bettis Field, Mckeesport PA, said Hendershot has made application for a patent for the new type motor in his own name, and will not submit it under an option to any group.

To Confer With Lindy

The partner explained, however, that Hendershot would do nothing with his motor without consulting the Guggenheim foundation and his attorney. Hendershot is scheduled to go to New York today to consult with Col. Charles A. Linbergh, Maj. Thomas G. Lanphier, noted fliers, and his attorney, Col. Henry Breckenridge, also Col. Lindbergh's attorney. Members of the Guggenheim foundation will be at the conference, Peet said.

Peet said Hendershot was not interested in the development of his motor for purposes other than aviation, primarily.

"Of course these other things, such as developing energy for other purposes will come later; they will have to, but for the time being Hendershot is interested only in the advancement of aviation," Peet explained.

A variational approach to probing extreme events in turbulent dynamical systems

Abstract

Extreme events are ubiquitous in a wide range of dynamical systems, including turbulent fluid flows, nonlinear waves, large-scale networks, and biological systems. We propose a variational framework for probing conditions that trigger intermittent extreme events in high-dimensional nonlinear dynamical systems. We seek the triggers as the probabilistically feasible solutions of an appropriately constrained optimization problem, where the function to be maximized is a system observable exhibiting intermittent extreme bursts. The constraints are imposed to ensure the physical admissibility of the optimal solutions, that is, significant probability for their occurrence under the natural flow of the dynamical system. We apply the method to a body-forced incompressible Navier-Stokes equation, known as the Kolmogorov flow. We find that the intermittent bursts of the energy dissipation are independent of the external forcing and are instead caused by the spontaneous transfer of energy from large scales to the mean flow via nonlinear triad interactions. The global maximizer of the corresponding variational problem identifies the responsible triad, hence providing a precursor for the occurrence of extreme dissipation events. Specifically, monitoring the energy transfers within this triad allows us to develop a data-driven short-term predictor for the intermittent bursts of energy dissipation. We assess the performance of this predictor through direct numerical simulations.
