VR and AR wearable Arduino glove keyboard

December 16, 2017

Glove keyboards have been in the common imagination for decades.

He was eager as he touched the switch. His glasses flickered to life. Once again
his fingers carved into the empty air and began clicking together. ... 
The contacts on his fingers connected, and the letters appeared in the
lens of his glasses like ghosts in the air.
-- excerpt from "Digital Fortress" (1998) by Dan Brown

Her fingers twitch in conversation with her exo-nervous system—the silver wires
filigreeing her fingers.
-- excerpt from "Hel's Bet" (2016) by Doug Sharp

However currently there is somewhat of a void in the wearable keyboard department. While many attempts have been made over the years with a variety of keyers, few have met with any success.

TAP wearable

The most successful thus far is the TAP wearable, though successful in this instance means an effective marketing campaign. It has a previous iteration that also had good marketing but never made it into customers hands. This time there are at least some beta-testers. While they promise to start shipping this month, we’ll see what happens.

The TAP is a very complicated piece of proprietary machinery. While I can’t vouch for it’s reliability, in general the more complex something is, the more often it brakes.

Also I haven’t read any sci-fi featuring the TAP, or any kind of tapping on your leg kind of keyboards. In all the examples I saw people were sitting down, or tapping on a surface, which is different from the typical mobile use-case of people walking and standing.

Another issue with the TAP, is that being a single-handed thing, much like the KeyGlove it is probably limited to 32 or so input letters. Meaning it is only really useful for texting, and not for formatting documents, gaming or computer programming.

Simple Glove Keyboards

My proposed alternative is a very simple wearable glove keyboard.

Here is an example prototype description

  1. Take a pair of anti-static polyester gloves
  2. Sew conductive fabric onto all the finger tips
  3. Sew an independent conductive connection to each finger tip, with snap connectors at the wrist. 4 Repeat for both hands.
  4. Snap a 6 wire ribbon (with 5 live wires) to the glove,
  5. connect the thumbs to ground, and the fingers to digital pins with input high..
  6. with a bluetooth module, and a little software it’s a keyboard, where each hand is responsible for 4 bits. A 1 is when finger touches ground, indexfinger is lowest place.

The Ctrl, Alt and Super keys are the only ones that may need special treatment, as may have to press them, click some other letter, and then press them again to release.

Otherwise it’s all pretty straight keyboard stuff. May also need to put in some time delay, in case not all the contacts hit at the same time.

Mouse Control

Since standard keyboard codes start at 8, can do mouse controls underneath.

For VR, 3, 6, and 7 can be left, right, and middle click respectively.

For AR, 1, 2, 4, 5 can be left, right, down and up respectively. Can follow the keynav or a more traditional style with acceleration based on preference.

Software

The easiest to implement would be using standard keycodes, that way would have maximum compatibility with ordinary keyboards.

0x41 (ring finger touching thumb on left hand, and index touching thumb on right hand) would be ‘a’.

0x42 (same with left hand, and middle touching thumb on right) ‘b’ 0x43 (same left, both index and middle touching thumb on right) ‘c’

and so on.

An alternative would be to use direct encodings like ASCII or UTF16 for input Though may need to do some arduino side conversion so that it would appear to the computer to be a standard keyboard. The alternative would be making a whole new driver which would be much more work. Though the joy2chord seems like it may be possible to use as a basis, at least for Linux based systems.

UTF16 based system would be most useful for Chinese, since they have around 3000 common chracters. All of which could be entered with “two keystrokes”, or touching fingers together twice in appropriate arrangement.

Most other languages that have less than 67 letters alongside the standard numbers and symbols would be able to fit in a single touch.
