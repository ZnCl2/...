Algorithms to Live By

August 25, 2018

Algorithms to Live By: The Computer Science of Human Decisions by Brian Christian
My rating: 5 of 5 stars

This is a very dense book, full of exciting ways of improving ones life using computer science insights.
Here is a quick summary of the chapters:

1. Optimal Stopping: When to stop looking?
After you’ve seen 37% of the candidates.

2. Explore/Exploit: The Latest vs the Greatest
When lots of time then can explore for new options,
when less time can exploit or make use of what has already been discovered.
Young people tend to be more exploration heavy,
and older people more exploitation heavy,
but it all depends on perception,
if young person told they have short time to live they exploit,
and if old person told they have long time to live they explore.

Again, when do you decide on what park to frequent?
When you’ve explored 37% of the ones in your area.

3. Sorting: Making order

Covers various sorting methods, IIRC concludes one of the best (for clothing) is batch sort,
where you break up a large pile into small piles, and sort those.

4. Caching: Forget about it

Is about how can avoid having to sort clothes at all,
simply by having a caching system,
where most recently used clothing goes in laundry bin or dresser near bed,
if that bin is full, the clothing that is least likely to be used gets put in closet.
if the closet is full then the least likely to be used clothing gets put in basement,
and so-on, using the hierarchy of caches.

Can do it for other things, like tools, cutlery, files.
Best way to sort files is based on most recently used first.

5. Scheduling: First Things First

To get the most done, do the shortest length item first.
To avoid procrastination divide item length based on it’s weight/importance.
To avoid priority inversion, if a low importance task must be completed before
a high importance task can get started, then it inherits the high priority.

6. Baye’s Rule: predicting the future

There is a simple formula for probability, called Laplace’s Law:
number of successes plus one divided by number of attempts plus two.
probability=(successes+1)/(attempts+2).

7. Overfitting: When to Think Less

When you have a very little information about something
then should keep things simple.

Or the complexity of your plan/approach should correspond
to the amount of information you have (probably <37%).

Cross-validate with new information, and penalize complexity.

8. Relaxation: Let it slide

Talks about how certain problems are intractable in their strict form,
however if we simplify the constraints then can make a solution,
this allows for finding out something approaching the ideal.

9. Randomness: When to leave it to chance.

For certain problems using randomness achieves as good or faster results
as more deterministic approaches.

The idea is to take random samples of a complex organization to get a picture of what is going on.
Also in terms of making something can use several randomized prototypes.

With Simulated Annealing can lower the amount of randomness in each prototype as you get closer to the goal.

10. Networking: How We Connect

Mostly talks about computer network protocols,
with a few suggestions about how to apply them to life.

11. Game Theory: The Minds of Others

Mentions that the common prisoners dilemma is mostly a problem of how the game is designed,
and that with a different design the prisoners could be expected to co-operate.
Such as if they had a Godfather who made it quite clear the snitch would die.

Concludes that it’s best to play games rigged for honesty.

12. Computational Kindness

Talks about how it’s important to think of the cognitive load you are putting on people,
for example that it’s better to say what you want rather than politely deferring,
since simulating what someone else wants is a massive cognitive load.


In Conclusion this is a great book, usually I just get the ebooks,
but with this book I also ordered a hard copy for my bookshelf.
